How to use competitor reviews to improve your restaurant

Your competitors' bad reviews are the cheapest market research you will ever do. Customers tell you, in writing, exactly what they wish a similar restaurant did differently. Most operators only read their own reviews. The ones who read competitors' reviews systematically — and translate what they find into actual changes — pull ahead within a couple of seasons.

This guide is a practical method, not a theory piece. By the end, you should have a repeatable monthly routine that turns competitor reviews into menu, service and pricing decisions.

Step 1 — Identify your real comp set

The mistake here is choosing competitors by category ("other Italian restaurants") rather than by overlap ("places the same guest would also consider when they pick us"). The right comp set is usually five to seven venues that share at least two of: cuisine, price point, neighbourhood, and occasion.

A short exercise: think about your last 20 reservations. What other restaurant would each of those guests have considered if you were fully booked? List those names. The ones that appear repeatedly are your real competitors.

For a small Athens neighbourhood bistro, the comp set might be:

  • The other neighbourhood bistro two streets away (same cuisine, same price).
  • The wine bar that does small plates around the corner (different cuisine, same occasion).
  • The hotel restaurant five minutes away (different vibe, similar price).
  • The newer place that opened six months ago (same demographic).
  • The reliable older place locals default to (the incumbent you measure against).

You do not need ten. Five well-chosen competitors is enough to spot patterns.

Step 2 — Set up the reading routine

Decide where you read from. The three useful sources are:

  1. Google reviews — most volume, fastest signal.
  2. TripAdvisor — longer reviews, more thoughtful, often tourists.
  3. Reservation platforms (TheFork, OpenTable, local equivalents) — reviews only from verified diners, more reliable.

Once a month, set aside 90 minutes. Pull up each competitor's page. Read the last 20-30 reviews. Specifically focus on the 2- and 3-star reviews. Five-star reviews tell you what they do well. Two-star reviews tell you where they leak guests — and that is where you have an opportunity.

Step 3 — Tag systematically

Unstructured reading produces unstructured ideas. Tag what you read into five buckets:

BucketWhat to look forWhy it matters
Menu complaintsDishes mentioned negatively, missing options, dietary frustrationsDirect menu-engineering input
Service complaintsWait times, attentiveness, knowledge of menu, languageTraining and floor-plan signal
Value complaintsPortion sizes, bottled water charge, cover charge, wine markupPricing strategy input
Atmosphere complaintsNoise, lighting, smell, music volume, temperatureEasy fixes you can make this week
Booking complaintsReservation difficulty, no-show treatment, deposit policyOperations and reservation policy input

A simple Google Sheet with five columns works. Add a sixth column for which competitor said it, and a seventh for whether it overlaps with your own reviews.

Step 4 — Look for patterns, not anecdotes

A single guest saying "the pasta was overcooked" tells you almost nothing. Three competitors all getting hit on "slow service on Sunday lunch" tells you Sunday lunch is the structural problem in your neighbourhood. That is a positioning opportunity — if you can crack Sunday service while everyone else slips, you own a daypart.

The patterns worth acting on usually meet three tests:

  1. Frequency. The same issue appears in at least three competitors' last 30 reviews.
  2. Recency. It is showing up in the last 60 days, not in old reviews.
  3. Specificity. The reviewers describe the problem in detail, not in general terms. "Tomato sauce was sweet to the point of jam" is a specific signal; "food was okay" is not.

Step 5 — Translate signals into changes

This is where most operators stop. They read competitor reviews, they nod, and they go back to running their own kitchen. Translating signals into changes requires committing to one or two concrete actions per month.

Three examples of how this plays out:

Example 1 — The seafood-overcooked pattern

Three competitors in a coastal town keep getting hit on overcooked octopus and dry sea bass. Action: instead of competing on price or portion, the operator quietly reworks her sea bass cooking method to a lower temperature longer hold, then makes "how we cook our fish" a menu story. Reviews start mentioning it within two months.

Example 2 — The wine markup pattern

Four of five competitors are flagged in 2-star reviews for wine pricing being "insulting". Action: a Limassol bistro introduces a tighter, more transparent wine list with three accessible house bottles under €30 and a wine-by-the-glass programme that is honest about supplier. Within a season, value mentions in their own reviews flip positive.

Example 3 — The Sunday service pattern

The whole comp set struggles with Sunday lunch attentiveness — one guest in seven complains. Action: the operator staffs Sunday lunch with the same team every week (not the weakest rotation), trains them specifically on Sunday rhythm, and adds a Sunday-only set menu that simplifies the kitchen load. Sunday becomes the highest-NPS service of the week.

Step 6 — Do it without copying

The point is to learn, not to imitate. Imitation is obvious to guests and exhausting to staff. Three rules to keep on the right side of the line:

  • Solve the problem your way. If competitors are weak on service speed, do not just hire more servers. Look at your floor plan, your handover, your menu structure.
  • Do not copy menu items name-for-name. If you spot a dish that is being requested in competitor reviews and not delivered, build your own version with your ingredients and your story.
  • Do not poach by reading reviews of staff. Reviews sometimes name competitors' best staff. Approaching them based on that is unprofessional and small-town news travels.
A single guest saying "the pasta was overcooked" tells you almost nothing. Three competitors all hit on "slow Sunday lunch" tells you Sunday is the opportunity.

Step 7 — Track whether it worked

Tie your changes to your own review trends. Three months after introducing a fix:

  1. Is the equivalent topic now appearing positively in your own reviews?
  2. Has your average score in that topic shifted vs the comp set?
  3. Are you mentioned in reviews of competitors ("better than [you] down the road")?

If yes to any of these, the loop is working. If none, your fix probably wasn't the right fix or wasn't executed visibly enough.

How long this should take

A realistic monthly cadence:

  • Week 1. 90 minutes reading competitor reviews, tagged into the spreadsheet.
  • Week 2. 30 minutes spotting patterns and choosing 1-2 actions.
  • Weeks 2-4. Execute the actions.
  • End of month. 30 minutes reviewing your own review trends.

Three hours a month, total. The compounding effect across a season is what makes it worth doing.

Bottom line

Pick five real competitors, not ten convenient ones. Read their 2- and 3-star reviews monthly. Tag systematically. Look for patterns, not anecdotes. Pick one or two changes per month. Solve the problem your own way. Track the result. The operators who do this consistently for a year end up running quietly better restaurants than the ones who only watch their own page.

Frequently asked

Questions, answered.

How many competitors should I monitor?
Five to seven. Below five, you do not see patterns. Above seven, you stop reading carefully and start skimming. Pick by actual guest overlap, not by category.
Should I include national chains in my comp set?
Only if a meaningful share of your guests would actually choose between you and the chain. A neighbourhood trattoria does not need to monitor a casual-dining chain three kilometres away unless guest interviews show real overlap.
Is it legal to read competitors' public reviews?
Yes. Public reviews are public. Scraping at industrial scale can breach platform terms of service, but a manager reading a handful of competitors' pages every month is straightforward research.
What if my competitors have very few reviews?
Then competitor review analysis is not your highest-yield activity. Spend the time on your own reviews and on direct guest conversations instead.
How often should I refresh the comp set?
Twice a year. New restaurants open, others close, guest demographics shift. A list that has not been revisited in 18 months is probably out of date.
