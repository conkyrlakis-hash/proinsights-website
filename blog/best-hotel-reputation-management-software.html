Best hotel reputation management software for 2026

Hotel reputation software is a more mature category than the restaurant equivalent. The big four — Shiji ReviewPro, TrustYou, Revinate and Customer Alliance — have been around for a decade or more. They are good tools. They are also priced for chains, sold on long contracts, and often more than an independent hotel actually needs.

This piece is an honest side-by-side of the six platforms an independent or small-group hotelier in Europe would realistically shortlist in 2026, with what each is good at, where each falls down, and a practical view on price and contract length.

Shiji ReviewPro

What it does well. Probably the deepest analytics in the category. Strong semantic tagging across more languages than most competitors. Survey module integrates with the review side. Owned by Shiji, so well-aligned with hotels already on Shiji PMS / POS. Used by global chains.

Where it falls short. Cost. Long contracts. Implementation usually takes weeks. The UI has improved but is still designed for analysts, not duty managers. If you have one or two properties and you want a duty manager to see today's reviews in two minutes, ReviewPro is over-engineered for that job.

Fit. Chains and groups, especially those already in the Shiji ecosystem.

TrustYou

What it does well. TrustYou's score is one of the few independent meta-scores OTAs actually display, which gives the tool a moat. Strong on aggregating reviews into a normalised score and on language coverage. Survey module is solid.

Where it falls short. The product is built around the meta-score; if you are not interested in surfacing a TrustScore widget on your website or driving OTA ranking through it, you are paying for something you may not use. Pricing skews mid-to-high. Sales process is enterprise-style.

Fit. Chains that already lean into TrustYou's ranking score, or independents that specifically want to push the TrustScore as a credibility signal.

Revinate

What it does well. Revinate's email marketing module is the strongest in the hotel category — segmentation, automation, deliverability. The reputation module is genuinely useful when bundled with the CRM/email stack.

Where it falls short. If you only want reviews, Revinate is the wrong tool. It is priced and sold as a full guest data platform. Implementation is a project. The reputation module on its own is competent but not best-in-class.

Fit. Hotels that want guest CRM and email marketing first, with reputation included.

Customer Alliance

What it does well. European mid-market sweet spot. Decent balance between survey collection and review aggregation. Pricing is more reasonable than the US-influenced competitors. German engineering culture shows in the dashboard.

Where it falls short. Less polished in non-European languages. UK and Irish coverage thinner than ReviewPro or TrustYou. Onboarding requires more effort from the hotel than the lighter tools.

Fit. Independent and small-group hotels in continental Europe, particularly Germany, Austria, Italy and the Benelux.

MARA

What it does well. Built specifically for AI-drafted replies. The draft quality is good. Sentiment tagging is accurate. Light implementation. Monthly pricing.

Where it falls short. Narrow scope. Does not do competitor benchmarking, surveys, or guest messaging. You still need a way to know reviews have arrived.

Fit. Hotels that want a focused reply-drafting tool layered on top of native review back ends.

ProInsights

What it does well. Daily WhatsApp and email digest is the differentiator. Built for managers who do not log into dashboards. Cheap entry tier. Greek, Cypriot and broader Mediterranean coverage handled natively. Draft replies are short and operator-friendly. Competitor benchmarking included.

Where it falls short. Smaller team than ReviewPro or TrustYou. Integration list is shorter. No native survey tool yet. Not designed for a 200-property enterprise rollout.

Fit. Independent and small-group hotels (1-20 properties), especially in Europe and the Eastern Mediterranean.

How to choose without getting locked in

The biggest mistake independent hoteliers make in this category is signing a 24-month contract for an enterprise tool before they have tested how it fits the daily routine. Five practical rules:

  1. Start with a 30-day pilot, not a contract. Reputable vendors will agree to one. If they will not, that is information.
  2. Pilot with a real duty manager, not the owner. The owner uses dashboards. The duty manager uses notifications. The duty manager's experience predicts whether the tool will stick.
  3. Test the language coverage on your worst-language reviews. A tool that handles English flawlessly and butchers Greek or Italian will quietly degrade your reply quality.
  4. Ask for an itemised quote. "€X per month all-in" hides things. Survey modules, additional users, additional properties, API access — each has a price.
  5. Negotiate annual, not monthly, but only after the pilot. Annual is usually 10-20% cheaper. Multi-year is usually only 5% on top of that and locks you in.

What's actually changed in 2026

Three things worth flagging:

  • AI-drafted replies are now standard. Every credible vendor offers them. Quality has converged. The differentiator is no longer "do you have AI replies" but "are they short, on-brand, and editable in two clicks".
  • WhatsApp delivery is becoming the asked-for channel. Email digests still dominate, but managers under 40 increasingly want the morning summary on WhatsApp. Most enterprise vendors still do not offer this.
  • OTA review weighting has shifted. Booking.com's review prompt timing has changed, leading to higher response rates and slightly lower average scores across the industry. This is normal variance, not a problem with your hotel.

Common procurement mistakes

A short list of things we see hoteliers regret a year into the contract:

  • Buying the survey module before testing the review module. The survey module is often the most expensive line item. Most hotels do not use it well. Take the review tool first, add surveys only if your response volume is high enough to justify them.
  • Letting marketing pick the tool without the duty manager in the room. The CMO uses dashboards. The duty manager uses notifications. If the duty manager finds the tool annoying, it dies within six months.
  • Signing 24 months for the sales discount. The 12-month discount is usually close enough. Long contracts only make sense after a successful first year.
  • Ignoring language coverage. A hotel in Cyprus or Greece where 30% of reviews are in Greek needs a tool that handles Greek well. Most enterprise tools handle it acceptably; some struggle. Test before signing.

Bottom line

The right tool depends on size and where you sit in the chain-versus-independent spectrum. ReviewPro and TrustYou are the safest enterprise picks. Customer Alliance is the strongest European mid-market option. ProInsights and MARA are the most reasonable starting points for an independent hotel that does not need a 24-month commitment. Frequently asked

Questions, answered.

What is the difference between ReviewPro and TrustYou?
ReviewPro leans on depth — semantic tagging, surveys, integrations with Shiji's hotel stack. TrustYou leans on its meta-score that OTAs display, which makes it useful as a credibility signal as well as a monitoring tool. Both are priced for chains.
Can I cancel a reputation software contract early?
Usually not without a penalty. Most enterprise contracts in this space are 12-24 months with auto-renewal. Always read the cancellation window — typically 60-90 days before renewal — and diarise it.
Do I need a survey tool as well as a review tool?
Only if you want to capture feedback from guests who would not leave a public review. Survey responses are private, lower-friction, and more honest. They do not replace public reviews, but they catch issues earlier.
Will the software integrate with my PMS?
ReviewPro, TrustYou and Revinate integrate with the major PMSs (Opera, Mews, Cloudbeds, Shiji). Lighter tools like ProInsights and MARA usually do not need PMS integration to monitor reviews — they pull from the public review platforms directly.
How important is WhatsApp delivery?
More important than vendors realise. In our experience, managers who get a daily WhatsApp summary read 85-90% of digests within an hour. Managers on email digests read maybe 40-50% the same day. The channel matters.
