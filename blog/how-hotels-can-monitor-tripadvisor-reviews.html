How to monitor TripAdvisor reviews daily

TripAdvisor is still the platform most independent hotels under-monitor. Booking.com gets attention because it drives reservations. Google gets attention because it shows up in search. TripAdvisor sits in the middle: high-intent, comparison-heavy readers, and reviews that compound over years rather than weeks.

This guide covers what you can monitor for free, where TripAdvisor's native tools stop being enough, and how to set up a daily routine that actually fits the way hotel front-office teams work.

What TripAdvisor gives you natively

If you have claimed your property in the Management Center, you have access to a fixed set of free tools. They are useful, but you need to know their limits before you build a routine on top of them.

ToolWhat it doesWhere it falls short

The free daily setup any property can run

You do not need to pay for a tool to monitor TripAdvisor properly. You do need 15 minutes a day and a few habits. Here is the minimum viable setup:

  1. Turn on review email notifications in the Management Center. Send them to a shared inbox, not one manager's personal email — otherwise the system breaks the moment that person is on leave.
  2. Create a Google Alert for the exact name of your hotel plus the word "tripadvisor". This catches forum threads, blog posts and articles that link to your page — not just reviews.
  3. Bookmark your Management Center and open it at the same time every morning. Pair it with the front-office handover.
  4. Keep a one-page log. Date, reviewer, score, one-line summary, who is replying, status. A simple Google Sheet is enough.
  5. Reply within 48 hours. TripAdvisor's algorithm rewards response rate. So do prospective guests reading the page.

This setup costs nothing and works. It also has obvious failure modes: it relies on one person doing the same thing every day, it does not survive holidays well, and it gives you no view of competitors.

When the free setup stops being enough

You will know it is time to add a paid tool when one or more of these things start happening:

  • You are missing reviews for two or three days at a stretch because the notification email got filtered.
  • You have more than one property and the routine does not scale (multiplying by even three hotels makes the spreadsheet collapse).
  • Your owner or asset manager wants competitor benchmarking — "how are we doing vs the four similar hotels down the road" — and you cannot answer with TripAdvisor alone.
  • Senior staff are spending an hour a day on review reading and reply drafting, and you can see the cost.
  • You want a single view across TripAdvisor, Booking.com and Google rather than logging into three back ends.

What the next tier of tools actually does

The category is sometimes called "reputation management" or "review intelligence". Tools in this space do four things, in roughly this order of usefulness:

  1. Aggregation. Pull every review from every platform into one inbox. This alone usually pays for the tool.
  2. Alerting. Push notifications by email, WhatsApp or app when a low-score review appears, ideally within an hour.
  3. Sentiment tagging. Automatically tag reviews by topic (breakfast, cleanliness, staff, noise) and sentiment. Most tools do this with a language model now.
  4. Benchmarking. Compare your scores to a competitive set you define.

Higher tiers add survey tools, post-stay email automation, and CRM integrations. Most independent hotels do not need those at first.

A daily routine that works at scale

Here is the routine we see at independent hotels that handle 200-400 reviews a year well, across two or three platforms:

07:30 — Front-office reads daily review digest before the shift handover.
           Flags any 1-3 star review for the duty manager.

09:30 — Duty manager checks the flagged reviews against the night audit,
           housekeeping log, and any complaints from the previous day.

10:00 — Duty manager drafts replies. Anything sensitive goes to the GM.

14:00 — Replies posted on TripAdvisor, Booking.com, Google.
           One-line summary added to the daily ops log.

End of week — GM reviews 7-day digest: score average, response rate,
              recurring topics. Maximum 30 minutes.

The whole routine takes around 45-60 minutes a day spread across three people. The trick is the morning digest. Without one consolidated read, the front office ends up clicking between three tabs and misses things.

What to actually track week to week

Most hotel reputation dashboards show too many metrics. Five are enough:

  1. Average score, last 30 days vs prior 30. Direction matters more than absolute.
  2. Response rate. Below 80% is a red flag, particularly on TripAdvisor.
  3. Review volume. Sudden spikes or drops usually correlate with a real event (a tour group, a TV mention, a maintenance issue).
  4. Top three negative topics. Same three weeks in a row = a real operational problem.
  5. Competitor delta. Your score minus the average of three named competitors. Easier to communicate to owners than absolute numbers.
TripAdvisor reviews compound over years rather than weeks. A bad month bleeds into next season's ranking.

Common mistakes

  • Replying in templates only. Readers spot it. Use a base template, change at least two sentences per reply.
  • Ignoring positive reviews. Replying to negatives only makes the page look defensive. Reply to a sample of 5-star reviews each week.
  • Asking for reviews in person at check-out. Better to ask 24-48 hours after departure by email, when memory is positive but not pressured.
  • Letting the routine die on holidays. Reviews come in every day. Set up a delegation rota for August and December.

Three signals that the routine is breaking

Independent hotels rarely make a clean decision to upgrade tooling. The decision usually creeps up on them. Three signals to watch for:

  1. Reviews you only find out about a week later. Either the notification email is being filtered, or the manager on the rota that week stopped checking. Either way, the routine has a single point of failure.
  2. Replies that all start to sound the same. When the same person has been replying for a year, fatigue shows in the language. Future readers notice.
  3. Owner asking for benchmarking and you cannot answer. "How are we doing vs the four similar properties down the road" is a question that TripAdvisor's free back end cannot answer cleanly.

When to pay for software

The simplest rule: if you are spending more than four hours a week reading and copying review data between platforms, a paid tool will save you money. Bottom line

Monitoring TripAdvisor daily is a process problem, not a software problem. Most independent hotels can do it well for free with discipline. The point at which software becomes worth paying for is the point at which you stop being able to look at all platforms in the same place every morning — and the point at which the routine starts to depend on one person remembering to check, every day, without exception.

Frequently asked

Questions, answered.

Does TripAdvisor charge to claim my property?
No. Claiming the listing and using the Management Center is free. You only pay if you opt into TripAdvisor's paid Business Advantage subscription, which adds features like a contact button and photo placement, but does nothing for monitoring.
How long does TripAdvisor take to publish a review?
Usually 24-72 hours. Reviews are pre-screened by an automated system, with manual review for borderline cases. Some get rejected for breaching guidelines (third-party submission, employee involvement, off-topic content).
Can I delete a bad TripAdvisor review?
Only if it breaches TripAdvisor's content guidelines. Use the "Report this review" link in the Management Center, pick the relevant guideline, and provide evidence. Pure complaints, even unfair ones, almost never get removed.
How often does TripAdvisor's Popularity Ranking update?
Daily, but the algorithm weights recent reviews, response rate, and quantity across a long window. Big single-day moves are unusual unless your competitive set has changed.
What is a good response rate on TripAdvisor?
Above 80% is healthy for an independent hotel. Above 95% is normal for chains and properties using a reputation tool. Below 50% is a visible weakness on the page.
