Why your restaurant rating dropped and what to check first

The first instinct when your average rating drops is to panic. The second is to blame the platform. Both are wrong. Most rating drops are explainable in 30 minutes with a structured diagnostic. This guide gives you that diagnostic.

By the time you have worked through the checklist below, you will know whether the drop is a measurement artefact, a single-event problem, a structural operational issue, or something else entirely.

Step 1 — Confirm the drop is real

Before anything else, sanity-check the number. A small restaurant with 200 lifetime reviews can show a 0.2-star monthly drop from a single bad week. That is not a trend, it is statistical noise.

Three things to check:

  1. What is the window? A 7-day rolling average will swing wildly. Use a 30- or 90-day window for anything you intend to act on.
  2. How many reviews underpin the new average? Below 10 reviews in the window, the swing tells you almost nothing.
  3. Is the lifetime score also down? If only the recent window is down but the lifetime is stable, you have a short-term issue, not a structural one.

Step 2 — Which platform fell first?

The platform sequence matters. Different platforms have different reviewer profiles, so a drop that starts on one and spreads to another tells you something different from a drop that hits everywhere at once.

PatternLikely cause
Google drops first, then TripAdvisor follows two weeks laterLocal clientele is unhappy. The locals review Google first; tourists catch up on TripAdvisor later.
TripAdvisor drops, Google stableSpecific tourist segment (cruise, tour group, foreign-language guests) had a bad run.
Booking.com drops, Google and TripAdvisor stableHotel-restaurant guests are unhappy. Often a breakfast or set-menu issue.
All three drop together within a weekSingle high-impact event (a service night, a kitchen issue, a viral social moment).
One platform drops, others riseAlmost certainly a measurement artefact — reviewer mix has changed, not service quality.

Step 3 — Read the actual reviews, not the score

The score is a symptom. The text is the diagnosis. Pull the last 15 reviews that pushed the average down. Read each one with three questions in mind:

  1. Which dish or service moment is named? Tag every specific mention.
  2. Which staff member, if any, is named? Positive or negative.
  3. What time and day did the visit happen? Most reviews mention this.

Write the answers in a three-column list. After 15 reviews, patterns are usually obvious to the eye. If "slow service" appears in 11 of 15 reviews, you have your answer. If 15 reviews each complain about something different, your problem is more diffuse.

Step 4 — The weekend-vs-weekday test

Tag each negative review by day of week. If the drop is concentrated on Friday and Saturday, you have a capacity or scheduling problem. If it is concentrated on Monday and Tuesday, you may have a chef-off / weakest-shift problem. If it is evenly spread, the issue is structural.

A real example. A bistro in Limassol saw its score fall from 4.5 to 4.2 over six weeks. Eight of the eleven 2-star reviews were Saturday dinner. The fix turned out to be staffing — the strongest server had left two months earlier and Saturday handovers were now sloppy. Once they restaffed Saturday specifically, the score recovered within a season.

Step 5 — The dish-by-dish test

If specific dishes are named more than three times across your sample, look at the kitchen change history. Did you change supplier? Change prep? Change the chef on that station? Reviewers often spot ingredient changes that even regular guests miss for a few weeks.

A diagnostic question we have seen catch issues fast: "in the last 60 days, what has changed in our kitchen — even something we considered trivial?" Examples that have caused real rating drops:

  • Switched bread supplier to a cheaper one.
  • Changed olive oil for service from one used in the kitchen.
  • New dishwasher detergent leaving residue on glassware.
  • Fryer oil change schedule pushed from daily to every 36 hours.
  • New POS system causing slower table turn.

Step 6 — Is the bigger market also down?

If your competitors' scores are also falling, the cause might be external. The summer tourist mix shifted. A heatwave made the city uncomfortable. A new platform UX is prompting more reviews (and reviewer mix shifts always show up in the score).

Pull the 30-day averages of three competitors with comparable volume. If they are down too, your drop is partially industry-wide. If only yours is down, the problem is you. Both are useful to know.

Step 7 — The three most common root causes

From hundreds of conversations with operators, the most common explanations for a sustained rating drop are:

  1. A key staff member left, and the replacement isn't yet at the same level. Usually a senior server or sous chef. Effect shows up 4-8 weeks after departure. Recovery takes the time needed to train the replacement, plus a season for the reviews to refresh.
  2. A quiet supplier or recipe change you considered minor. Bread, butter, oil, coffee, fries. Guests notice the staples faster than the dishes you spent time on.
  3. Service speed under capacity pressure. Reservations have grown 20% but the floor plan and rota haven't changed. The 8.30pm Saturday sitting is now structurally too tight, and reviews start mentioning waits.
The score is a symptom. The text is the diagnosis. Pull the last 15 reviews and read them with three questions in mind.

The 30-minute diagnostic

If you do nothing else, work through this in order:

  1. Confirm the drop is real over a 30- or 90-day window with at least 15 reviews in it.
  2. Identify which platform moved first.
  3. Pull the last 15 negative reviews. Tag dish, staff, day of week.
  4. Spot any pattern of three or more.
  5. List every kitchen, service or supplier change in the last 60 days.
  6. Compare your 30-day average to three competitors' to rule out market shift.
  7. Choose the single most likely root cause. Commit to one specific fix this week.
  8. Re-check in 30 days.

What not to do

  • Do not respond defensively to every negative review. You'll make a drop visible to every reader. Reply carefully and offline where possible.
  • Do not ask staff to solicit positive reviews to dilute the score. Platforms detect review-stuffing and your overall ranking suffers more than the average score gains.
  • Do not blame the platform's algorithm. It is almost never the algorithm.
  • Do not change five things at once. If you cannot tell which fix worked, you cannot tell which problem you had.
  • Do not over-discount as a recovery tactic. Cutting prices to drive volume just brings in a more price-sensitive guest who is statistically more likely to leave a critical review. You make the trend worse.
  • Do not blame your front-of-house team without checking the kitchen first. In our experience, more than half of "service problems" trace back to kitchen pacing or ticket times, not to the floor team.

A short note on social media drops

Occasionally a rating drop is not driven by visit experience at all, but by a social media moment — a viral TikTok, a Reddit thread, a negative news mention. These are easy to spot: the volume of new reviews spikes and the reviewers are disproportionately first-time reviewers with no other history. If this is your pattern, treat it as a PR situation, not an operational one. Reply calmly to the legitimate visit reviews, flag the brigading reviews to the platform, and wait it out. These drops almost always self-correct within 60-90 days as real-visit reviews return to dominating the feed.

Bottom line

A rating drop is rarely random. It is almost always explainable by reading 15 reviews carefully and asking what changed in the last 60 days. The operators who panic blame the platform. The ones who quietly recover do the diagnostic.

Frequently asked

Questions, answered.

How big a drop is significant?
A change of 0.1-0.2 stars over a month is usually noise for a small restaurant. A change of 0.3+ over 90 days with at least 20 reviews in the window is real and worth investigating.
Can Google's algorithm cause a rating drop on its own?
Google occasionally adjusts how reviews are weighted (e.g. recency weighting) which can shift displayed scores slightly across the board. But a sustained drop of more than 0.2 stars is rarely algorithmic — it is almost always real reviewer feedback.
Should I temporarily stop asking for reviews if the score is dropping?
No. Stopping requests starves the score of the positive reviews that would balance it. Keep asking, but make sure you are also fixing the underlying issue, otherwise you accelerate the trend.
How long does it take a rating to recover?
Roughly the same time it took to fall, sometimes longer. A 30-day drop typically takes 60-90 days of consistent improvement to fully reverse, because old negative reviews keep weighting the average.
Should I respond to old negative reviews I missed?
Yes, even months later. A thoughtful late reply is better than no reply. It shows current readers that you take feedback seriously, regardless of when it was written.
