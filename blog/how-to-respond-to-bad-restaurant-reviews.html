How to respond to bad restaurant reviews without making it worse

Every restaurant manager has stared at a one-star review on a Monday morning and felt the same thing: a hot, defensive urge to type back. That urge is the problem. The reply most owners draft at 9am is the one that ends up screenshotted on a local Facebook group by 11am.

This guide walks through how to respond to bad reviews on Google, TripAdvisor and Booking.com without making the situation worse. It covers three real-world reply templates you can adapt, the phrases to keep out of your responses, and the legal pitfalls that catch operators off guard when they push back on a guest in public.

Why the reply matters more than the review

Most prospective diners do not read your worst review. They read the reply underneath it. A calm, specific, non-defensive reply often reassures readers more than a flawless review history. A snarky reply, on the other hand, becomes the headline.

Two practical reasons to take this seriously:

  • Replies are public forever. You can edit them on most platforms, but the original is often cached and screenshotted within hours.
  • Replies are how new diners measure you. A reader scanning your page in 90 seconds will form a view of your professionalism from your last five replies, not your last fifty reviews.

The 24-hour rule

Do not reply within the first two hours. Do not reply the same day if you are still angry. Most platforms give you up to a month before the response window starts to feel slow. Use 24 hours to verify what actually happened.

Before drafting anything, work through this short checklist:

  1. Pull the booking, table or reservation. Confirm date, time, server, table number.
  2. Ask the server and the manager on duty for their version. Write it down.
  3. Check the kitchen ticket time and any POS notes (comped items, sent-back dishes).
  4. Check whether the same guest has reviewed competitors recently. Their tone elsewhere is useful context.
  5. Decide: is this a legitimate complaint, an exaggeration of something real, or a fake review?

Template 1 — One-star service complaint (legitimate)

The guest waited too long, the server was rude, the bill was wrong. This is the most common category. The reply has three jobs: acknowledge specifically, take responsibility without grovelling, offer a route to make it right offline.

Hello [First name],

Thank you for taking the time to write this. I'm the manager on duty for [day]. I've gone back through the service that evening with our team and you're right — the wait between courses was too long, and the way the bill was explained was sloppy. That's on us, not you.

We've already changed how we hand over tables between the bar and the dining room on busier evenings, because this wasn't the first time it has happened.

If you're willing, I'd like to invite you back as my guest so we can show you what the evening should have looked like. You can reach me directly at [email] or [phone].

— [Your name], Manager

Why this works: it names the failure precisely (wait between courses, the bill handover), it does not blame the guest, it shows that something has already changed internally, and it moves the conversation offline.

Template 2 — Two-star food complaint (partially fair)

Sometimes the guest is half-right. The dish was off that night, but they have also embellished. Do not argue the embellishment in public. Address the part that is true, leave the rest alone.

Hi [First name],

I'm sorry the [dish name] didn't land for you on [date]. I've spoken to the kitchen — the sauce was reduced too far that service, and that explains the texture you've described. We've adjusted the prep card so it doesn't happen again.

On the noise level, I can only say that we run a small open-kitchen room and it's part of the atmosphere. It isn't for every evening, and I appreciate that didn't suit you on the night.

If you'd like to try us again on a quieter weekday, please email me at [email] — I'd be glad to put you on a corner table.

— [Your name]

This reply concedes the kitchen mistake, refuses to apologise for the room concept (which is not a fault), and offers a concrete alternative.

Template 3 — Suspected fake review

If you cannot match the review to any booking, the description does not match any dish you serve, or the reviewer's profile only has negative one-star reviews for competing restaurants, you may be looking at a fake. Do not say "this is a fake review" in public, ever. Instead, reply for the reader, then flag it to the platform.

Hello,

Thank you for the feedback. We take every review seriously, but I have to be honest — we've checked our reservation system, kitchen tickets and CCTV for the date you mention and we have no record of this visit, and the dishes you describe aren't on our menu.

If you did dine with us and we've missed it, please email me at [email] with a name or a card receipt so we can look into it properly. Otherwise, we'll continue to be judged by the guests who do visit us.

— [Your name], Manager

Then, separately, use the platform's reporting tool. Google's "Flag as inappropriate" works best when paired with a written follow-up to Google Business Profile support citing the policy you believe was breached (off-topic, conflict of interest, fake engagement).

Legal pitfalls operators run into

A few things to keep in mind, particularly in the EU:

  • GDPR. You cannot confirm publicly that someone was a customer if they used a pseudonym. "Our records show you ordered the lamb" is technically a personal-data disclosure.
  • Defamation works both ways. Calling a reviewer a liar in writing, on a public page, can give them a cause of action against you. The bar is low in some jurisdictions.
  • Employee names. Never name a server publicly in a negative context. If a guest names them, do not repeat the name in your reply.
  • Health and safety claims. If the review alleges food poisoning, do not respond with "this could not have happened" — that becomes evidence later. Acknowledge, move the conversation to email, and document everything internally.

A simple weekly process

Replying to bad reviews well is mostly a calendar problem. Operators who do it consistently tend to follow a routine like this:

  1. Monday morning, 15 minutes. Read every review from the past week. Tag each as: kitchen, service, value, atmosphere, booking, fake.
  2. Tuesday, 30 minutes. Draft replies for everything 3 stars and below. Sleep on them.
  3. Wednesday. Post the replies. Forward the operational takeaways to the kitchen and floor managers.
  4. End of month. Look at the tags. If "service" is the biggest category three months running, the problem is not the reviewers.

If you don't want to log into Google, TripAdvisor and Booking.com separately every Monday morning, a daily review summary by WhatsApp or email is the simplest way to keep this routine alive. Bottom line

The goal of a public reply is not to win the argument. It is to make every future reader think: "I'd eat there. They take this seriously." Three honest sentences usually beat three angry paragraphs.

Frequently asked

Questions, answered.

How long should I wait before replying to a bad review?
At least a few hours, ideally 24. Long enough to verify the facts internally and to stop being emotional, short enough that the reply still feels timely. Most platforms do not penalise replies posted within a month.
Should I reply to every review or just the bad ones?
Reply to every review at one and two stars, every detailed review at three, and a sample of positive ones. Replying only to negatives makes your page look defensive.
Can I get a fake review removed?
Sometimes. Google, TripAdvisor and Booking.com all have policies against off-topic content, conflict of interest, and fake engagement. Flagging works better when you can show the reviewer's history (e.g. only negative reviews of competing venues) and explain which specific policy was breached.
Is it safe to offer a free meal in my reply?
Offer something privately, not publicly. A public offer turns into a price list for complaints and can also breach platform guidelines on incentivising reviews.
What if the review names a staff member?
Do not repeat the name in your reply. Address the behaviour or service issue in general terms, then handle the staff side internally. Naming staff publicly is a privacy issue and bad management.
