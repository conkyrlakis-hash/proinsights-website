Google review reply templates every restaurant should have

The right Google review reply takes two minutes to write if you have a template, twenty minutes if you start from scratch every time. The trick is to keep a personal template library — fifteen lines of your own — that you adapt rather than re-write. This article gives you ten templates as a starting point, plus the localisation rules that make them sound like you and not like a chain.

One caveat upfront: never paste a template verbatim. Readers can spot copy-paste replies within five seconds, and Google's algorithm appears to penalise pages where every reply reads identically. Change at least two sentences per reply. Always.

The 10 templates by rating × scenario

1. Five stars, generic positive (no specifics mentioned)

Hi [First name],

Thank you for taking the time to leave us five stars — we appreciate it. It means a lot to the kitchen and floor team to hear visits are going well.

Looking forward to seeing you again soon.

— [Your name]

When to use: 5-star reviews with no detail. Keep it short. Long replies to generic 5-stars look performative.

2. Five stars, specific praise (named a dish or server)

Hi [First name],

Really glad the [dish name] hit the mark — it's one [Chef]'s favourites on the menu. I'll pass on your kind words to [server first name] as well; they'll be pleased.

Thank you for taking the time to write this. See you again soon.

— [Your name]

When to use: 5-star reviews with a specific compliment. Naming a dish or staff member in your reply (when the reviewer did first) makes the reply feel real.

3. Four stars with a small criticism

Hi [First name],

Thank you for the kind review and for the honest feedback on [specific issue mentioned]. You're right — we should have caught that on the night, and we've fed it back to the team this morning.

We'd love to have you back so we can show you the full experience the next time.

— [Your name]

When to use: 4-star reviews where the guest praised you but flagged something. Acknowledge the criticism, do not deflect.

4. Three stars, mixed review

Hi [First name],

Thank you for the balanced feedback. I'm pleased the [positive thing they mentioned] worked for you, and I'd like to take the [negative thing they mentioned] seriously. I've gone back through the service that day with the team — you're right that the [specific moment] wasn't where it should have been.

If you'd be open to coming back, please email me directly at [email] and I'll make sure your next visit is on me.

— [Your name], Manager

When to use: 3-star reviews. These are the most important ones to reply to well, because future readers use them to gauge how you handle imperfect visits.

5. Two stars, food complaint (legitimate)

Hi [First name],

I'm sorry the [dish name] wasn't right on [date]. I've spoken to the kitchen and you're correct — the [specific element, e.g. sauce, cooking time] wasn't where it should have been. We've adjusted the prep card this week to stop it happening again.

If you'd be willing to give us another chance, please contact me at [email]. I'd like to invite you back as my guest.

— [Your name], Manager

When to use: 2-star reviews where the food complaint is specific and credible.

6. Two stars, service complaint

Hi [First name],

Thank you for the honest review, even though it's hard to read. I've checked the booking and spoken with the team on duty that evening. There's no excuse for the wait time you experienced, and the way the bill was explained was sloppy.

We're working on the handover between the bar and the dining room on busier nights, because this hasn't been the first time.

If you're open to it, I'd like to invite you back. You can reach me directly at [email].

— [Your name], Manager

When to use: 2-star reviews on service. Specificity matters. "We've checked the booking" beats "we're sorry to hear that" every time.

7. One star, dish-specific complaint

Hi [First name],

I'm very sorry about your experience with the [dish]. I've pulled the kitchen ticket from that evening and spoken to the chef on the station. The portion you received wasn't to standard — that's our error, not your perception.

I'd like to make this right properly. Please email me at [email] and I'll arrange for you and your guests to return as our guests, or refund the cost of the meal — whichever you prefer.

— [Your name], Manager

When to use: 1-star reviews with a clear, specific food complaint. Offer a concrete remedy privately.

8. One star, broad complaint ("everything was terrible")

Hi [First name],

I'm sorry your evening was that disappointing. From your review I can see you weren't happy, but I'd like to understand more specifically what went wrong so we can address it properly. Could you email me directly at [email] with a bit more detail — even just the time you visited and one or two specific moments — and I'll come back to you within 24 hours.

Thank you for taking the time to write, and I hope we can do better next time.

— [Your name], Manager

When to use: 1-star reviews with no specifics. The goal is to move the conversation offline so you can either fix it or, if it turns out to be a misunderstanding, address it without arguing on a public page.

9. One star, suspected fake / no record of visit

Hello,

Thank you for the feedback. I have to be honest — I've checked our reservation system and our card receipts for the date you mention, and we have no record of this visit. The dishes you describe also aren't on our menu.

If you did dine with us and we've missed it, please email me at [email] with a card receipt or booking name and we'll look into it properly.

Thank you,
— [Your name], Manager

When to use: reviews that look fake. Never accuse — state what you have and have not been able to verify. Then flag the review separately to Google.

10. One star, complaint about something out of your control

Hi [First name],

Thank you for the feedback. I'm sorry [external factor — parking, weather, noise from the street] affected your evening. We don't control that side of the experience, but I do want to acknowledge it took something away from your visit.

If you're open to coming back, please reach me at [email] and I'll do what I can to make sure it's a better evening.

— [Your name]

When to use: 1-star reviews about things you genuinely don't control. Acknowledge the impact, do not list excuses.

Localisation tips

Templates need to sound like your restaurant and your country. Three practical rules:

  1. Reply in the language the reviewer wrote in. If a guest reviewed in Greek, reply in Greek. If they reviewed in English, reply in English. Do not reply in both — it looks corporate.
  2. Use your house tone. If your restaurant addresses guests by first name on the floor, your replies should too. If you are more formal, keep replies more formal. Consistency matters.
  3. Localise specifics. A bistro in Limassol mentioning "the team on Anexartisias" sounds more real than "the team on duty". Use one or two locally-grounded specifics in your replies — staff first name, dish name in the local form, day of the week as locals refer to it.

What to never paste

Line to avoidWhy
"We are sorry you feel that way."Reads as an apology for the guest's emotions, not the issue. Patronising.
"This is not who we are."It is, on that day, by their experience. Argues with the guest in public.
"As you can see from our other reviews…"Defensive. Asking the reader to discount this one.
"With respect, your version of events is incorrect."Almost always a defamation risk. Always reads badly.
"We have CCTV footage that shows…"Threatening. GDPR-problematic. Never invoke CCTV in a public reply.
Reviewer's last name, employer or appearancePrivacy breach. Reads as intimidation even if not intended.
Same opening line on every replyPattern detection by Google and by readers. Vary your openings.

How to deploy templates without sounding canned

Two practical techniques:

  1. Vary the opening and closing. Keep the middle paragraph templated. Change the first and last sentence every time.
  2. Always include one specific. Name a dish. Name a day. Mention the chef's name. One concrete reference per reply moves it from "copy paste" to "real reply".
Readers can spot copy-paste replies within five seconds. Change at least two sentences per reply. Always.

How long to take per reply

Once you have a template library you trust, target two minutes per reply for 4- and 5-star reviews, five minutes for 3-star, and ten minutes for anything 2-star or below. Anything taking you longer than that is a sign the template is wrong or the case needs to be handled offline.

Bottom line

Templates work. Templates pasted verbatim do not. Build a library of fifteen of your own, varying tone and structure across rating bands, then change two sentences per reply when you use them. Reply in the reviewer's language, with your house tone, with one local specific. Never accuse, never threaten, never invoke CCTV. Two minutes per reply on the easy ones, ten on the hard ones, and you have a reputation operation that works.

Frequently asked

Questions, answered.

Should I reply to every Google review?
Yes for any review at 3 stars or below, every detailed review at 4 stars, and a sample of 5-star reviews each week. Replying only to negatives makes the page look defensive.
Can I edit a Google review reply after posting?
Yes. Edit through Google Business Profile. The edit is not visible to the public, only the latest version is shown. Use this when a reply needs softening on second reading.
How long should a Google review reply be?
Three to five sentences for most replies. Long replies look like you're arguing. Short replies look like you didn't read the review. Vary length deliberately.
Should I include emojis in my replies?
Almost never. Emojis read as flippant in a reply context, even when the reviewer used them first. The exception is when your restaurant brand voice is heavily emoji-based on social media — then be consistent.
Is it okay to ask for a follow-up review after fixing a complaint?
Better to ask for a private follow-up first. Once the guest is genuinely happy with the resolution, you can mention that they're welcome to update their review, but never pressure. Pressured edits are obvious and counter-productive.
